Similar to first talk, but stressing things that cannot be done in other languages. The only thing that immediately comes to mind is Macros in the various Lisps and Reader Macros in Common Lisp, but I am sure there are others.
Memoization as solution, for example, to complexity of pegpuzzle problem. How far does it get you? We talked about using a mutable hashmap behind the scenes while maintaining referential transparency externally. Is there a purely functional alternative that is not too clumsy?
Explicit advantages of functional programming languages. Are there advantages of imperative languages? Where do they diverge? Where do they overlap?

I also think it would be interesting to discuss the characteristics of Imperative, Object Oriented, and Functional languages. For one thing, I think the terms Object Oriented and Functional are orthoganol. For another, I believe many things are erroneously claimed as characteristics of one group or another.
See: Kent Pitman's Parenthetically Speaking column, "What's in a name?"
Frameworks The Java world has exploded into a collection of frameworks. Most serious organizations make use of something like Spring or Guice. Many use maven. To what extent are these good practices and to what extent are they not necessary in the functional world?
Design Patterns. Norvig and Graham have written that they consider them a bad sign. A lot of OO programmers have misunderstood them and turned them from a language into a list of "best practices," if not an out and out religion. Interestingly, many of the people who came up with them (e.g. Richard Gabriel) are situated pretty firmly in the Lisp community. To what extent are these patterns appropriate to functional design? To what extend does functional programming define its own patterns? (I would argue that monad and company are a good example of functional design patterns.)
AOP et al
Do trends like AOP have a place in the functional world? (Again, this came out of the Lisp community, with Gregor Kiczales and a lot of the Parc guys driving it.)
